Singly Reinforced Beams

IS 456 : 2000 Cl. 38 Flexure Design Design of rectangular beams with tension reinforcement only

1. Introduction

Definition
A singly reinforced beam is a rectangular RCC beam in which steel reinforcement is provided only in the tension zone (bottom of the beam for a simply supported span). No compression steel is provided. It is the simplest and most common type of beam in building construction.

When a beam bends under load, the top portion is in compression (concrete resists) and the bottom portion is in tension (steel resists). In a singly reinforced section, we rely entirely on the concrete to carry all compression and on the tension steel bars to carry all tension.

The design is governed by IS 456 : 2000, Clause 38 — Limit State of Collapse in Flexure.

2. Assumptions in LSM Flexure Design

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 38.1
The following assumptions are made for the Limit State of Collapse in flexure:
  1. Plane sections remain plane after bending — strain varies linearly across the depth.
  2. The maximum compressive strain in concrete at the outermost fibre is taken as 0.0035.
  3. The tensile strength of concrete is ignored — only steel carries tension.
  4. The stress in reinforcement is derived from the representative stress-strain curve for the type of steel.
  5. The compressive stress distribution in concrete is represented by the IS 456 parabolic-rectangular stress block (see Limit State Method page).
  6. The maximum strain in tension steel at the Limit State shall not be less than: $$\varepsilon_{su} = \frac{f_y}{1.15 \times E_s} + 0.002 = \frac{0.87 f_y}{E_s} + 0.002$$

4. Neutral Axis Depth

The neutral axis (N.A.) is the line where bending stress is zero — above it the concrete is in compression; below it the steel carries tension. By equating the compression and tension forces (force equilibrium):

$$C_u = T_u$$ $$0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot x_u = 0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st}$$ $$\boxed{x_u = \frac{0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st}}{0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b}}$$
Actual neutral axis depth — IS 456:2000, Cl. 38.1

This is used to find the neutral axis depth when $A_{st}$ is known (analysis problem). In a design problem, $A_{st}$ is the unknown we solve for.

5. Limiting (Maximum) Neutral Axis Depth

IS 456 imposes a maximum neutral axis depth $x_{u,\max}$ to ensure the beam fails in a ductile manner — i.e., the steel yields first (giving warning) before the concrete crushes. This is achieved by limiting the strain in steel at failure to a minimum value.

From the strain diagram using similar triangles, with concrete strain = 0.0035 and minimum steel strain $= 0.87f_y/E_s + 0.002$:

$$\frac{x_{u,\max}}{d} = \frac{0.0035}{0.0035 + \left(\dfrac{0.87 f_y}{E_s} + 0.002\right)}$$
Limiting neutral axis ratio — IS 456:2000, Cl. 38.1, Table G of SP 16

Critical Rule
The design must ensure $x_u \leq x_{u,\max}$. If the required $x_u$ exceeds $x_{u,\max}$, the section is over-reinforced and will fail by sudden concrete crushing — this is not permitted. The solution is to either increase the beam size or provide compression steel (doubly reinforced beam).

6. Moment of Resistance

The moment of resistance $M_u$ is the couple formed by the compression force $C_u$ and tension force $T_u$ separated by the lever arm $z$.

Taking moment about the tension steel:

$$M_u = C_u \times z = 0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot x_u \times (d - 0.42 \, x_u)$$
Moment of resistance — compression side

Taking moment about the compression resultant:

$$M_u = T_u \times z = 0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st} \times (d - 0.42 \, x_u)$$
Moment of resistance — tension side (IS 456 Cl. 38.1)

Limiting Moment of Resistance

When $x_u = x_{u,\max}$, the moment of resistance is maximum for a singly reinforced section:

$$M_{u,\lim} = 0.36 \, \frac{x_{u,\max}}{d} \left(1 - 0.42 \, \frac{x_{u,\max}}{d}\right) f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot d^2$$
Limiting moment of resistance — singly reinforced section

Design Tip
In a design problem, first check if $M_u \leq M_{u,\lim}$. If yes, a singly reinforced section is sufficient. If $M_u > M_{u,\lim}$, you need a doubly reinforced beam.

7. Under-Reinforced, Over-Reinforced, and Balanced Sections

Section TypeConditionFailure ModeAllowed by IS 456?
Under-Reinforced $x_u < x_{u,\max}$ Steel yields first → large deflection and cracking gives warning before collapse. Ductile failure. ✅ Yes — preferred in design
Balanced $x_u = x_{u,\max}$ Steel and concrete reach limiting strain simultaneously. Maximum efficiency. ✅ Yes — maximum singly reinforced capacity
Over-Reinforced $x_u > x_{u,\max}$ Concrete crushes suddenly before steel yields. Brittle failure — no warning. ❌ No — not permitted

8. Minimum and Maximum Steel

Minimum Tension Steel

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 26.5.1.1
Minimum area of tension steel in a beam:
$$\frac{A_{st,\min}}{b \cdot d} = \frac{0.85}{f_y}$$
Minimum steel to prevent sudden brittle failure of concrete on cracking

For Fe 415: $A_{st,\min} = \dfrac{0.85}{415} \times b \times d = 0.00205 \, b \cdot d$

For Fe 500: $A_{st,\min} = \dfrac{0.85}{500} \times b \times d = 0.00170 \, b \cdot d$

Maximum Tension Steel

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 26.5.1.1
Maximum area of tension or compression steel shall not exceed:
$$A_{st,\max} = 0.04 \times b \times D$$
Maximum steel — 4% of gross cross-sectional area. $D$ = overall depth.

9. Effective Depth and Cover

The effective depth $d$ is measured from the extreme compression fibre to the centroid of the tension reinforcement.

$$d = D - d'$$ $$d' = \text{clear cover} + \text{stirrup diameter} + \frac{\phi_{main}}{2}$$
Effective depth calculation. $\phi_{main}$ = diameter of main tension bars.

Thumb Rule for Preliminary Sizing
As a starting point, assume effective depth: $$d \approx \frac{\text{Span}}{10} \text{ to } \frac{\text{Span}}{12} \quad \text{(for simply supported beams)}$$ And width: $b \approx d/2$ to $d/3$. These are starting estimates — always verify by calculation.

10. Shear Design

Shear design is performed in addition to flexure design. The key checks are:

Nominal Shear Stress

$$\tau_v = \frac{V_u}{b \cdot d}$$
$V_u$ = factored shear force at the section (IS 456 Cl. 40.1)

Design Shear Strength of Concrete $\tau_c$

$\tau_c$ depends on the percentage of tension steel $p_t = \dfrac{100 A_{st}}{bd}$ and the grade of concrete. Values are given in IS 456 Table 19.

Shear Reinforcement

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 40.4
Shear reinforcement (stirrups) is designed for the excess shear:
$$V_{us} = V_u - \tau_c \cdot b \cdot d$$ $$\frac{A_{sv}}{s_v} = \frac{V_{us}}{0.87 \, f_y \, d}$$
$A_{sv}$ = area of stirrup legs; $s_v$ = stirrup spacing; use 2-legged vertical stirrups

Minimum Shear Reinforcement (IS 456 Cl. 26.5.1.6)

$$\frac{A_{sv}}{b \cdot s_v} \geq \frac{0.4}{0.87 \, f_y}$$
Minimum stirrup requirement even when $\tau_v < \tau_c$

11. Step-by-Step Design Procedure

For a simply supported singly reinforced rectangular beam under a given factored load.

  • Given Data Note down: span $l$, dead load $w_{DL}$, live load $w_{LL}$, grade of concrete $f_{ck}$, grade of steel $f_y$, exposure condition.
  • Calculate Factored Load and Design Moment
    $w_u = 1.5(w_{DL} + w_{LL})$ kN/m
    $M_u = \dfrac{w_u \, l^2}{8}$ (for simply supported beam with UDL)
  • Assume b and d; Check $M_{u,\lim}$ Assume $b$ (typically 230 mm or 300 mm). Assume $d$ using thumb rule. Calculate $M_{u,\lim} = R_{u,\lim} \cdot f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot d^2$. Verify $M_u \leq M_{u,\lim}$ — if not, increase $d$ or use doubly reinforced section.
  • Find Neutral Axis Depth $x_u$ Solving $M_u = 0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st} \cdot (d - 0.42 \, x_u)$ and $x_u = \dfrac{0.87 f_y A_{st}}{0.36 f_{ck} b}$ simultaneously gives:
    $A_{st} = \dfrac{0.36 \, f_{ck} \, b \, x_u}{0.87 \, f_y}$ where $x_u$ is found from the quadratic in $M_u$
  • Solve for $A_{st}$ Directly The standard design formula (derived from the above equations):
    $A_{st} = \dfrac{0.5 \, f_{ck}}{f_y} \left[1 - \sqrt{1 - \dfrac{4.6 \, M_u}{f_{ck} \, b \, d^2}}\right] b \, d$
    This is from IS 456 Annex G / SP 16.
  • Check Minimum and Maximum Steel Verify: $A_{st,\min} \leq A_{st} \leq A_{st,\max}$
  • Select Bar Diameter and Number Choose bar diameter $\phi$ and number of bars $n$ such that $n \times \dfrac{\pi \phi^2}{4} \geq A_{st}$. Check bar spacing and fit within width $b$.
  • Check Effective Depth Calculate actual $d = D - \text{cover} - \phi_{stirrup} - \phi_{main}/2$. Ensure it matches assumed $d$.
  • Check Span-to-Depth Ratio (Deflection) Verify $l/d \leq$ permissible value from IS 456 Cl. 23.2.1.
  • Shear Design — Find $V_u$, calculate $\tau_v$ and $\tau_c$, design stirrups $V_u = w_u \times l/2$ (for UDL on SS beam). Find $\tau_c$ from IS 456 Table 19. Design stirrups if needed.
  • Draw Reinforcement Detailing Sketch Show cross-section with bar positions, cover, stirrup size and spacing, and overall dimensions.

12. Worked Example

Problem Statement

Design a simply supported singly reinforced rectangular beam for the following data:

  • Clear span = 5.0 m
  • Dead load (excluding self-weight) = 15 kN/m
  • Live load = 20 kN/m
  • Grade of concrete: M25 ($f_{ck}$ = 25 MPa)
  • Grade of steel: Fe 415 ($f_y$ = 415 MPa)
  • Exposure condition: Mild
  • Width of beam: b = 300 mm (assumed)
Step 1 — Effective Span

Assume overall depth $D$ ≈ 550 mm, width $b$ = 300 mm.

Effective span $l$ = clear span + $d$ (lesser of support width and $d$) ≈ 5.0 + 0.5 = 5.0 m (taken as clear span for simplicity in preliminary design).

Step 2 — Self Weight and Factored Load

Assume $D$ = 550 mm, $b$ = 300 mm.

Self weight of beam = $0.30 \times 0.55 \times 25$ = 4.125 kN/m ≈ 4.13 kN/m

Total dead load $w_{DL}$ = 15 + 4.13 = 19.13 kN/m

Live load $w_{LL}$ = 20 kN/m

Factored load: $w_u = 1.5 \times (19.13 + 20.0) = 1.5 \times 39.13$

$w_u$ = 58.70 kN/m
Step 3 — Factored Bending Moment and Shear

For simply supported beam with UDL:

$M_u = \dfrac{w_u \cdot l^2}{8} = \dfrac{58.70 \times 5.0^2}{8} = \dfrac{58.70 \times 25}{8}$

$M_u$ = 183.44 kN·m

$V_u = \dfrac{w_u \cdot l}{2} = \dfrac{58.70 \times 5.0}{2}$

$V_u$ = 146.75 kN
Step 4 — Effective Depth and Limiting Moment Check

Clear cover (mild exposure) = 20 mm. Assume stirrups = 8 mm, main bars = 20 mm.

Effective cover $d'$ = 20 + 8 + 20/2 = 38 mm

Effective depth $d$ = 550 − 38 = 512 mm

For Fe 415: $x_{u,\max}/d$ = 0.479, so $R_{u,\lim}$ = 0.138

$M_{u,\lim} = 0.138 \times f_{ck} \times b \times d^2 = 0.138 \times 25 \times 300 \times 512^2$

$= 0.138 \times 25 \times 300 \times 262{,}144$

$M_{u,\lim}$ = 271.9 kN·m

Since $M_u$ (183.44 kN·m) $\leq$ $M_{u,\lim}$ (271.9 kN·m) ✓ — singly reinforced section is sufficient.

Step 5 — Area of Tension Steel $A_{st}$

Using the direct design formula:

$A_{st} = \dfrac{0.5 \times 25}{415} \left[1 - \sqrt{1 - \dfrac{4.6 \times 183.44 \times 10^6}{25 \times 300 \times 512^2}}\right] \times 300 \times 512$

Inner term: $\dfrac{4.6 \times 183.44 \times 10^6}{25 \times 300 \times 262144} = \dfrac{843.8 \times 10^6}{1966.1 \times 10^6} = 0.4293$

$\sqrt{1 - 0.4293} = \sqrt{0.5707} = 0.7555$

$A_{st} = \dfrac{0.5 \times 25}{415} \times (1 - 0.7555) \times 300 \times 512$

$= 0.03012 \times 0.2445 \times 153{,}600 = 0.03012 \times 37{,}555$

$A_{st}$ = 1131 mm²
Step 6 — Check Minimum Steel

$A_{st,\min} = \dfrac{0.85}{415} \times 300 \times 512 = 0.002048 \times 153{,}600$

$A_{st,\min}$ = 314 mm²

$A_{st}$ (1131 mm²) $>$ $A_{st,\min}$ (314 mm²) ✓

Step 7 — Select Bars

Try 4 bars of 20 mm diameter:

$A_{st,\text{provided}} = 4 \times \dfrac{\pi \times 20^2}{4} = 4 \times 314.16$

$A_{st,\text{provided}}$ = 1257 mm² > 1131 mm² ✓

Check bar fit: $b = 300$ mm. Min spacing = max(bar dia, 25 mm) = 25 mm. Space used = $4 \times 20 + 3 \times 25 + 2 \times 20 = 80 + 75 + 40 = 195$ mm $<$ 300 mm ✓

Step 8 — Deflection Check (Span/Depth Ratio)

Basic $l/d$ for simply supported beam = 20

Actual $l/d = \dfrac{5000}{512} = 9.77$

9.77 < 20 ✓ — Deflection is OK
Step 9 — Shear Design

$\tau_v = \dfrac{V_u}{b \cdot d} = \dfrac{146.75 \times 10^3}{300 \times 512} = \dfrac{146750}{153600}$

$\tau_v$ = 0.956 MPa

$p_t = \dfrac{100 \times 1257}{300 \times 512} = \dfrac{125700}{153600} = 0.819\%$

From IS 456 Table 19, for M25 and $p_t$ ≈ 0.82%: $\tau_c$ ≈ 0.57 MPa

Max shear $\tau_{c,\max}$ for M25 = 3.1 MPa (IS 456 Table 20) — section is OK.

Excess shear: $V_{us} = V_u - \tau_c \cdot b \cdot d = 146750 - 0.57 \times 300 \times 512 = 146750 - 87552$

$V_{us}$ = 59,198 N

Using 2-legged 8 mm stirrups ($A_{sv} = 2 \times 50.27 = 100.5$ mm²):

$s_v = \dfrac{0.87 \times 415 \times 100.5 \times 512}{59198} = \dfrac{18{,}774{,}355}{59198}$

$s_v$ = 317 mm — Provide 8 mm 2-legged stirrups @ 300 mm c/c

Max stirrup spacing = min(0.75d, 300 mm) = min(384, 300) = 300 mm ✓ (IS 456 Cl. 26.5.1.5)

Final Design Summary
  • Size: 300 mm × 550 mm (b × D)
  • Effective depth: 512 mm
  • Tension reinforcement: 4 bars of 20 mm dia (Fe 415) — $A_{st}$ = 1257 mm²
  • Stirrups: 8 mm dia, 2-legged, @ 300 mm c/c (Fe 415)
  • Clear cover: 20 mm (mild exposure)
  • All IS 456 checks satisfied ✓

13. Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the errors that most often cost marks in exams and cause re-work on site. Watch for them when applying this topic.

Common MistakeWhy it’s wrong / the correct approach
Allowing the section to become over-reinforced ($x_u > x_{u,\max}$).IS 456 does not permit this — failure would be sudden concrete crushing. If $M_u > M_{u,\lim}$, increase the depth or design a doubly reinforced section.
Forgetting the beam self-weight in the loads.Self-weight ($0.25\times b\times D$ in kN/m for normal concrete... i.e. $25\,b\,D$) is a dead load and must be added before factoring.
Using overall depth $D$ instead of effective depth $d$ in the moment equation.All flexure formulae use the effective depth $d = D - \text{cover} - \phi_{stirrup} - \phi/2$, measured to the centroid of tension steel.
Skipping the deflection (span/depth) check.A beam adequate in strength can still deflect excessively. Verify $l/d$ against the basic ratio (20 / 26 / 7) modified for steel percentage — IS 456 Cl. 23.2.1.
Using the wrong $x_{u,\max}/d$ for the steel grade.It is 0.479 for Fe 415 and 0.456 for Fe 500 — not a single fixed value. The limiting moment coefficient changes with it.
Designing with the service (unfactored) moment.Strength design uses the factored moment $M_u = 1.5\,(M_{DL}+M_{LL})$. Forgetting the 1.5 under-designs the section.

14. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between $M_u$ and $M_{u,\lim}$?

$M_u$ is the factored design moment from the loads; $M_{u,\lim}$ is the maximum moment a singly reinforced section of that size can resist. Design is valid only if $M_u \le M_{u,\lim}$.

Why are under-reinforced sections preferred?

The steel yields before the concrete crushes, giving large deflections and cracking as warning before failure — a ductile, safer mode.

What do I do if $M_u > M_{u,\lim}$?

Either increase the effective depth (or width), or keep the size and add compression steel — i.e. design a doubly reinforced beam.

Why is the design stress in steel $0.87 f_y$?

It is the characteristic yield strength divided by the material safety factor: $f_y/1.15 = 0.87 f_y$.

How do I choose trial $b$ and $D$?

As a start, $d \approx l/10$ to $l/12$ for simply supported spans and $b \approx d/2$ to $d/3$; then verify by calculation and the deflection check.

Does a singly reinforced beam still need top bars?

Yes — at least two hanger bars at the top to hold the stirrups, even though they are not counted in the flexure design.
