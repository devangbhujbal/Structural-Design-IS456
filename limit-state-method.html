1. Design Philosophy

Definition
A structure or a structural element is said to have reached a Limit State when it becomes unfit for use — either because it has collapsed (safety) or because it has deformed or cracked excessively (serviceability).

The Limit State Method (LSM) is the modern design philosophy adopted by IS 456 : 2000 for the design of reinforced concrete structures. It ensures that a structure remains fit for its intended purpose throughout its design life with an acceptable level of reliability.

The method accounts for the variability of both loads (using partial safety factors for loads, $\gamma_f$) and material strengths (using partial safety factors for materials, $\gamma_m$) in a rational and probabilistic manner.

IS 456 : 2000 Reference
Clause 18 — Bases for Design; Clause 36 — Limit State Design; Annex B — Stress-Strain Relationship for Concrete and Steel.

2. Working Stress Method vs Limit State Method

Before LSM, the older Working Stress Method (WSM) was used. Understanding the difference helps you appreciate why LSM was adopted.

Working Stress Method (WSM)

  • Stresses in materials kept below allowable (permissible) limits
  • Assumes linear elastic behaviour throughout
  • Uses a single factor of safety on material strength
  • Does not distinguish between dead load and live load variability
  • Conservative — leads to heavier, uneconomical sections
  • Governed by IS 456 (older editions) — rarely used now

Limit State Method (LSM) ✓ Preferred

  • Designs against actual failure (collapse) and loss of serviceability
  • Uses non-linear stress-strain relationships — more realistic
  • Separate partial safety factors for loads and materials
  • Accounts for different variability of dead, live, and wind loads
  • More economical — optimised section sizes
  • Adopted in IS 456:2000 — current standard method

4. Limit State of Collapse (LSC)

The Limit State of Collapse ensures that a structure has adequate strength and stability against collapse under the worst combination of loads during its service life. This is the primary limit state in structural design.

Design is carried out using factored loads (loads multiplied by partial safety factors $\gamma_f$) and design strengths (material strengths divided by partial safety factors $\gamma_m$).

$$\text{Design Strength} \geq \text{Design Load Effect (Factored)}$$ $$\frac{f_k}{\gamma_m} \geq \gamma_f \times F_k$$
The fundamental inequality of Limit State Design (IS 456:2000 Cl. 36.1)

Where:

  • $f_k$ = characteristic strength of material
  • $\gamma_m$ = partial safety factor for material
  • $\gamma_f$ = partial safety factor for load
  • $F_k$ = characteristic load

5. Limit State of Serviceability (LSS)

Even if a structure does not collapse, it may still be unfit for use if it deflects too much or cracks excessively. The Limit State of Serviceability checks are performed under service (unfactored) loads.

Check Limit as per IS 456 Clause
Maximum deflection (total) Span / 250 Cl. 23.2(a)
Deflection after construction (affecting partitions/finishes) Span / 350 or 20 mm (whichever is less) Cl. 23.2(b)
Crack width (mild exposure) 0.3 mm Cl. 35.3.2
Crack width (moderate/severe exposure) 0.2 mm Cl. 35.3.2
Practical Note
In most routine RCC design, deflection is controlled indirectly by limiting the span-to-effective depth ratio (IS 456 Cl. 23.2.1) rather than doing a full deflection calculation. This is the approach used in design examples on this site.

6. Partial Safety Factors for Loads ($\gamma_f$)

Different types of loads have different degrees of uncertainty. Dead loads are more predictable than live loads or wind loads. IS 456 specifies different $\gamma_f$ values for different load combinations.

IS 456 : 2000 — Table 18 (Cl. 36.4.1)
Partial safety factors for loads for Limit State of Collapse
Load Combination DL ($\gamma_{fDL}$) LL ($\gamma_{fLL}$) WL / EL ($\gamma_{fWL}$)
DL + LL 1.5 1.5 —
DL + WL/EL 1.5 (or 0.9*) — 1.5
DL + LL + WL/EL 1.2 1.2 1.2

* 0.9 is used when dead load contributes to stability against overturning or uplift.

DL = Dead Load, LL = Live Load, WL = Wind Load, EL = Earthquake Load

Most Common Case
For typical building floors and beams, the governing load combination is DL + LL with $\gamma_f = 1.5$. This is what we use in most beam and slab design examples. $$w_u = 1.5 \times (w_{DL} + w_{LL})$$

For Limit State of Serviceability

Serviceability checks use unfactored loads — i.e., $\gamma_f = 1.0$ for all load types.

7. Partial Safety Factors for Materials ($\gamma_m$)

Material strengths are also uncertain — concrete strength varies with mixing, curing, and testing. Steel has tighter quality control. IS 456 assigns:

$\gamma_c$
1.5
For Concrete
(IS 456 Cl. 36.4.2)
$\gamma_s$
1.15
For Steel
(IS 456 Cl. 36.4.2)

These give the design strengths actually used in calculations:

$$f_{cd} = \frac{0.67 \, f_{ck}}{\gamma_c} = \frac{0.67 \, f_{ck}}{1.5} = 0.446 \, f_{ck}$$
Design compressive strength of concrete. The factor 0.67 accounts for the difference between cube strength and cylinder (in-situ) strength.
$$f_{yd} = \frac{f_y}{\gamma_s} = \frac{f_y}{1.15} = 0.87 \, f_y$$
Design tensile strength of steel (IS 456:2000 Cl. 36.4.2)
Remember These Two Values
These are the two most used values in all RCC design calculations:
  • Design strength of concrete = 0.446 fck
  • Design strength of steel = 0.87 fy

10. Characteristic Loads and Strengths

Definition — Characteristic Strength
The characteristic strength $f_k$ is the value of strength below which not more than 5% of test results are expected to fall. It is defined statistically as: $$f_k = f_m - 1.65 \sigma$$ Where $f_m$ = mean strength, $\sigma$ = standard deviation of test results.
Definition — Characteristic Load
The characteristic load $F_k$ is the load which has a 95% probability of not being exceeded during the life of the structure. In practice, loads from IS 875 (Dead, Live, Wind) and IS 1893 (Seismic) are taken as characteristic loads.
Quick Summary of LSM Design Flow
  1. Determine characteristic loads (IS 875)
  2. Apply partial safety factors $\gamma_f$ → get factored (design) loads
  3. Calculate factored bending moment, shear, axial force
  4. Use design strengths $0.446 f_{ck}$ and $0.87 f_y$
  5. Ensure: Design Strength ≥ Factored Load Effect
  6. Check serviceability (deflection, cracking)
