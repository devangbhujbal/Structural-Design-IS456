1. What is a Two-Way Slab?

Definition
A two-way slab is a flat RCC slab supported on all four sides (on beams or walls) in which the ratio of the longer span to the shorter span is 2 or less ($l_y/l_x \leq 2$). It bends and spans in both directions, and reinforcement is provided in both the short and long directions.

Two-way slabs are more economical than one-way slabs for square or nearly square panels because the load is shared in both directions, resulting in smaller bending moments and thinner slabs. They are used in virtually all multi-storey building floor systems where the panel aspect ratio is close to 1.

IS 456 : 2000 — Annex D
The design of two-way rectangular slabs with simply supported or restrained (continuous/fixed) edges is covered in Annex D of IS 456:2000 using Rankine-Grashoff moment coefficients.

2. Structural Behaviour

Under load, a two-way slab deflects in a bowl shape. Load is transferred to all four supporting beams or walls. The shorter span carries more load than the longer span because it is stiffer.

Fig. 1 — Two-way slab with reinforcement in both directions. Short-span bars placed at bottom layer.

Short-span bars are placed at the bottom (outermost) layer of the tension zone because they carry the larger moment and need the greater lever arm.

Effective Depths for the Two Directions
Because one layer of bars sits on top of the other, the effective depths differ: $$d_x = D - \text{cover} - \frac{\phi_x}{2} \quad \text{(short span — bottom layer)}$$ $$d_y = D - \text{cover} - \phi_x - \frac{\phi_y}{2} \quad \text{(long span — top of bottom layer)}$$ Typically: $d_y = d_x - \phi_x$ (approximately $d_y \approx d_x - 10$ mm for 10 mm bars).

4. Nine Edge Conditions — IS 456 Annex D

IS 456 Annex D classifies two-way slabs into 9 cases based on edge support conditions. Each edge can be either simply supported (SS) or continuous (fixed/restrained). The case number determines which row of the moment coefficient table to use.

Case 1
All four edges simply supported
Case 2
One short edge discontinuous
Case 3
One long edge discontinuous
Case 4
Two adjacent edges discontinuous
Case 5
Two short edges discontinuous
Case 6
Two long edges discontinuous
Case 7
Three edges discontinuous (one long edge continuous)
Case 8
Three edges discontinuous (one short edge continuous)
Case 9
All four edges discontinuous (all simply supported)
Discontinuous vs Continuous Edge
A discontinuous (simply supported) edge is a free edge — the slab is supported but not built into the support (no moment restraint). A continuous edge is built into or monolithic with a beam or wall that provides rotational restraint, generating a negative (hogging) moment at that edge.

5. Depth of Slab

Depth is governed by deflection control using the basic span/depth ratio for the shorter span $l_x$:

Support ConditionBasic $l_x/d_x$ Ratio
Simply supported (all edges)20
One or more continuous edges26 (use for interior panels)
Cantilever7
$$d_x \geq \frac{l_x}{(l/d)_{\text{basic}}}$$ $$D = d_x + \text{clear cover} + \frac{\phi_x}{2}$$
Use shorter span $l_x$ for the depth calculation. Round $D$ up to the nearest 5 or 10 mm.

6. Design Moments Using IS 456 Coefficients

IS 456 Annex D uses the Rankine-Grashoff method, where moment coefficients $\alpha_x$ and $\alpha_y$ are tabulated for different $l_y/l_x$ ratios and edge conditions. The design moments per unit width are:

$$M_x = \alpha_x \cdot w_u \cdot l_x^2$$ $$M_y = \alpha_y \cdot w_u \cdot l_x^2$$
Design moments in short and long span directions — IS 456:2000, Annex D, Cl. D-1.1. Note: both use $l_x^2$, not $l_y^2$.
Both Moments Use $l_x^2$
A very common mistake is using $l_y^2$ for the long-span moment. Both $M_x$ and $M_y$ use $l_x^2$ (the shorter span squared) in IS 456 Annex D. The difference in moments between the two directions comes entirely from the different $\alpha$ coefficients.

Negative Moments at Continuous Edges

At continuous (restrained) edges, negative (hogging) moments also develop. IS 456 Annex D Cl. D-1.5 specifies:

$$M_{x,\text{neg}} = \frac{4}{3} \times M_x \quad \text{(negative moment at continuous short edge)}$$ $$M_{y,\text{neg}} = \frac{4}{3} \times M_y \quad \text{(negative moment at continuous long edge)}$$
Negative moment at a continuous edge = 4/3 × mid-span positive moment for that direction

8. Reinforcement Calculation

After finding $M_x$ and $M_y$, the steel in each direction is designed as a 1 m wide strip using the standard formula — exactly as for a one-way slab, but using the respective effective depth.

Short-Span Steel ($A_{stx}$) — using $d_x$:

$$A_{stx} = \frac{0.5 f_{ck}}{f_y}\left[1 - \sqrt{1 - \frac{4.6\,M_x}{f_{ck} \times 1000 \times d_x^2}}\right] \times 1000 \times d_x$$
Short-span steel per metre — placed at bottom layer

Long-Span Steel ($A_{sty}$) — using $d_y$:

$$A_{sty} = \frac{0.5 f_{ck}}{f_y}\left[1 - \sqrt{1 - \frac{4.6\,M_y}{f_{ck} \times 1000 \times d_y^2}}\right] \times 1000 \times d_y$$
Long-span steel per metre — placed above short-span layer

Minimum Steel (same as one-way slab):

$$A_{st,\min} = \frac{0.12}{100} \times 1000 \times D = 1.2D \quad \text{mm}^2\text{/m} \quad \text{(for Fe 415 / Fe 500)}$$

Spacing of Bars:

$$s = \frac{a_\phi \times 1000}{A_{st}} \leq \min(3d,\; 300\text{ mm})$$

9. Corner Reinforcement and Torsion Strips

At the corners of a two-way slab that are held down (i.e., corners that cannot lift), torsional moments develop in addition to the flexural moments. IS 456 Annex D Cl. D-1.8 requires special corner (torsion) reinforcement at such corners.

IS 456 : 2000 — Annex D, Cl. D-1.8
Torsion reinforcement shall be provided at corners where the slab is simply supported on both edges meeting at that corner. It is not required where both edges at the corner are continuous.
Fig. 2 — Torsion reinforcement zones at simply supported corners — IS 456 Annex D Cl. D-1.8

11. Step-by-Step Design Procedure

  • Check Two-Way Criterion $l_y/l_x \leq 2$ → two-way slab. Identify $l_x$ (shorter) and $l_y$ (longer) as c/c distances.
  • Identify Edge Condition Case (1 to 9) Check each of the four edges — simply supported (discontinuous) or continuous. Match to IS 456 Annex D Table 26 case number.
  • Assume Slab Thickness $D$ $d_x \approx l_x / 20$ (SS) or $l_x / 26$ (continuous). Add cover + bar radius to get $D$. Round up to nearest 10 mm.
  • Calculate Effective Depths $d_x = D - \text{cover} - \phi_x/2$; $d_y = d_x - \phi_x$ (approximately).
  • Calculate Factored Load $w_u$ $w_u = 1.5(25D + \text{finishes} + \text{LL})$ kN/m².
  • Read $\alpha_x$ and $\alpha_y$ from IS 456 Table 26 For the identified case number and computed $l_y/l_x$ ratio. Interpolate if needed.
  • Calculate Design Moments $M_x = \alpha_x \cdot w_u \cdot l_x^2$; $M_y = \alpha_y \cdot w_u \cdot l_x^2$. For continuous edges: $M_{\text{neg}} = \frac{4}{3} M_{\text{pos}}$.
  • Check $M_x \leq M_{u,\lim}$ $M_{u,\lim} = 0.138 \, f_{ck} \times 1000 \times d_x^2$. If exceeded, increase $D$.
  • Calculate $A_{stx}$ and $A_{sty}$ Use standard 1 m strip formula with respective effective depth. Check minimum steel.
  • Find Spacing of Bars in Each Direction $s = (a_\phi \times 1000)/A_{st}$. Check max spacing $= \min(3d,\; 300)$ mm.
  • Torsion Reinforcement at Corners Check corner conditions. Provide $\frac{3}{4}A_{stx}$ in zone $\frac{l_x}{5} \times \frac{l_y}{5}$ at SS corners — top and bottom.
  • Shear Check, Deflection, and Detailing Sketch Shear: $\tau_v = V_u/(1000 d_x)$ using $V_u = w_u l_x/2$. Apply $k$ factor. Draw plan and section.

12. Worked Example

Problem Statement

Design a two-way slab for the following data:

  • Panel size (c/c of beams): 4.0 m × 5.0 m
  • All four edges are simply supported (discontinuous) — Case 1
  • Live load = 3.0 kN/m²
  • Floor finish = 1.0 kN/m²
  • Concrete: M20 ($f_{ck}$ = 20 MPa)
  • Steel: Fe 415 ($f_y$ = 415 MPa)
  • Exposure: Mild (clear cover = 15 mm)
Step 1 — Check Two-Way Criterion

$l_x = 4.0$ m (short); $l_y = 5.0$ m (long)

$l_y / l_x = 5.0/4.0 = 1.25 \leq 2.0$ ✓

Two-way slab — proceed. Edge case: Case 1 (all four SS).
Step 2 — Slab Thickness

$d_x = l_x / 20 = 4000/20 = 200$ mm (SS basic ratio)

Assume main bar $\phi_x = 10$ mm. $D = 200 + 15 + 5 = 220$ mm → round to $D = 125$ mm

Wait — let us check if 200 mm effective depth makes sense for a 4 m span slab. Typical slabs are much thinner. Using modification factor $M_f$ ≈ 1.4 for low steel percentage:

$d_x = 4000 / (20 \times 1.4) = 4000/28 = 143$ mm → $D = 143 + 15 + 5 = 163$ mm → round to $D = 165$ mm

$D$ = 165 mm; assume $M_f$ = 1.4 (to be verified)

$d_x = 165 - 15 - 5 = $ 145 mm; $d_y = 145 - 10 = $ 135 mm

Step 3 — Factored Load

Self-weight = $25 \times 0.165 = 4.125$ kN/m²

Floor finish = 1.0 kN/m²; LL = 3.0 kN/m²

$w_u = 1.5 \times (4.125 + 1.0 + 3.0) = 1.5 \times 8.125$

$w_u$ = 12.19 kN/m²
Step 4 — Moment Coefficients (Case 1, $l_y/l_x = 1.25$)

Interpolate between $l_y/l_x = 1.2$ ($\alpha_x = 0.084$, $\alpha_y = 0.059$) and $l_y/l_x = 1.3$ ($\alpha_x = 0.093$, $\alpha_y = 0.055$):

For $r = 1.25$: $\alpha_x = 0.084 + 0.5 \times (0.093 - 0.084) = 0.084 + 0.0045$

$\alpha_x$ = 0.0885; $\alpha_y$ = 0.057 (interpolated)
Step 5 — Design Moments

$M_x = \alpha_x \cdot w_u \cdot l_x^2 = 0.0885 \times 12.19 \times 4.0^2 = 0.0885 \times 12.19 \times 16$

$M_x$ = 17.25 kN·m/m

$M_y = \alpha_y \cdot w_u \cdot l_x^2 = 0.057 \times 12.19 \times 16$

$M_y$ = 11.12 kN·m/m
Step 6 — Check $M_{u,\lim}$

$M_{u,\lim} = 0.138 \times 20 \times 1000 \times 145^2 = 0.138 \times 20 \times 1000 \times 21025$

$M_{u,\lim}$ = 58.03 kN·m/m

$M_x$ (17.25) $\ll$ $M_{u,\lim}$ (58.03) ✓

Step 7 — Short-Span Steel $A_{stx}$

Inner term = $\dfrac{4.6 \times 17.25 \times 10^6}{20 \times 1000 \times 145^2} = \dfrac{79.35 \times 10^6}{420.5 \times 10^6} = 0.1887$

$A_{stx} = \dfrac{0.5 \times 20}{415}(1 - \sqrt{1-0.1887}) \times 1000 \times 145$

$= 0.02410 \times (1 - 0.9012) \times 145000 = 0.02410 \times 0.0988 \times 145000$

$A_{stx}$ = 345 mm²/m

Min steel = $1.2 \times 165 = 198$ mm²/m. Provided $A_{stx}$ (345) > min (198) ✓

Spacing of 10 mm bars: $s = (78.54 \times 1000)/345 = 228$ mm → Provide 10 mm @ 220 mm c/c; $A_{stx,\text{prov}}$ = 357 mm²/m ✓

Step 8 — Long-Span Steel $A_{sty}$

Using $d_y = 135$ mm; $M_y = 11.12$ kN·m/m

Inner term = $\dfrac{4.6 \times 11.12 \times 10^6}{20 \times 1000 \times 135^2} = \dfrac{51.15 \times 10^6}{364.5 \times 10^6} = 0.1403$

$A_{sty} = 0.02410 \times (1 - \sqrt{0.8597}) \times 135000 = 0.02410 \times 0.0730 \times 135000$

$A_{sty}$ = 237 mm²/m

$A_{sty}$ (237) > min (198) ✓. Spacing of 10 mm bars: $s = 78.54 \times 1000/237 = 331$ mm > 300 mm limit → use 10 mm @ 300 mm c/c; $A_{sty,\text{prov}}$ = 262 mm²/m ✓

Step 9 — Torsion Reinforcement at Corners

All four corners are SS-SS → full torsion steel required at all four corners.

Zone size: $l_x/5 \times l_y/5 = 800$ mm $\times$ 1000 mm

Steel: $\frac{3}{4} \times A_{stx} = 0.75 \times 345 = 259$ mm²/m — in both directions, top and bottom.

Provide 8 mm @ 190 mm c/c ($A_{st}$ = 265 mm²/m) in both directions at all 4 corners, top and bottom, in zone 800 mm × 1000 mm
Step 10 — Shear Check

$V_u = w_u \times l_x / 2 = 12.19 \times 4.0/2 = 24.38$ kN/m

$\tau_v = 24380 / (1000 \times 145) = 0.168$ MPa

$p_t = 100 \times 357/(1000 \times 145) = 0.246\%$. From IS 456 Table 19, M20: $\tau_c \approx 0.35$ MPa

$k = 1.20$ (for $D = 165$ mm, interpolating IS 456 Cl. 40.2.1)

$k\tau_c = 1.20 \times 0.35 = 0.42$ MPa > $\tau_v$ (0.168 MPa) ✓ — No shear reinforcement needed
Final Design Summary
  • Slab thickness $D$: 165 mm | $d_x$: 145 mm | $d_y$: 135 mm
  • Short-span steel ($l_x$ direction, bottom): 10 mm @ 220 mm c/c — 357 mm²/m
  • Long-span steel ($l_y$ direction, above short-span): 10 mm @ 300 mm c/c — 262 mm²/m
  • Corner torsion steel: 8 mm @ 190 mm c/c — both directions, top and bottom, in 800 × 1000 mm zone at all 4 corners
  • Clear cover: 15 mm (mild exposure)
  • All IS 456 checks satisfied ✓
