1. What is a Flanged Beam?

Definition
A flanged beam is formed when a floor slab is cast monolithically with the supporting beam. A portion of the slab on either side of the beam web acts as a flange, contributing additional compression area. This makes the beam far more efficient than a plain rectangular section.

In a typical RCC floor system, beams and slabs are cast together. Under positive bending (sagging), the top flange is in compression and is very effective. The beam is then treated as a T-beam or L-beam rather than a plain rectangular beam — ignoring the flange would lead to a very conservative (over-designed) and uneconomical section.

When to Use Flanged Beam Design
Use T-beam or L-beam design when the slab and beam are cast monolithically (at the same time) and the beam is subjected to positive (sagging) bending moment. At supports of continuous beams where the moment is negative (hogging), the flange is in tension and is ignored — the section is designed as a rectangular web section.

3. Effective Flange Width — IS 456 Cl. 23.1

The full width of the slab does not act as the flange — only a limited width on each side of the web is effective in carrying compression. IS 456 Cl. 23.1.2 gives the rules for calculating the effective flange width $b_f$.

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 23.1.2
For T-beams, the effective flange width shall be the least of:

For T-Beams (Interior Beams):

$$b_f = \frac{l_o}{6} + b_w + 6 D_f$$
Effective flange width of T-beam — take the least of this, actual flange width, and c/c distance between adjacent beams

For L-Beams (Edge Beams):

$$b_f = \frac{l_o}{12} + b_w + 3 D_f$$
Effective flange width of L-beam — take the least of this, $b_w + $ half clear distance to adjacent beam, and actual flange width
SymbolMeaning
$b_f$Effective (design) flange width
$b_w$Breadth of web (rib width)
$D_f$Thickness of flange (= slab thickness)
$l_o$Distance between points of zero moment (≈ 0.7 × effective span for continuous beams; = effective span for SS beams)
Practical Note
Always compute $b_f$ by all three criteria and take the minimum. In many practical cases the $l_o/6 + b_w + 6D_f$ formula governs; in narrow bays the centre-to-centre spacing governs.

5. Two Cases — Neutral Axis Position

The entire analysis and design of a flanged beam depends on where the neutral axis falls relative to the flange thickness $D_f$. There are two cases:

Case 1 — N.A. in the Flange
($x_u \leq D_f$)

  • The full compression zone lies within the flange
  • The web is entirely in tension (ignored for flexure)
  • Section behaves as a rectangular beam of width $b_f$ and depth $d$
  • Simpler calculation — use rectangular beam formulae with $b = b_f$
  • This is the most common case in practice

Case 2 — N.A. in the Web
($x_u > D_f$)

  • Compression zone extends into the web below the flange
  • The stress block is no longer uniform across the width
  • Full T-section analysis required — two compression sub-areas
  • IS 456 uses a modified flange thickness $y_f$ to simplify the stress block
  • Occurs in heavily loaded beams with thin flanges
How to Determine the Case
First assume $x_u \leq D_f$ and compute $x_u$ using rectangular beam formula with $b = b_f$. Then check: if $x_u \leq D_f$ — Case 1 is confirmed. If $x_u > D_f$ — you must use Case 2.

6. Case 1 — Neutral Axis in the Flange ($x_u \leq D_f$)

When the neutral axis lies within the flange, the web below the flange is entirely in tension and is ignored. The section is treated exactly like a rectangular beam of width $b_f$.

Force Equilibrium:

$$0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b_f \cdot x_u = 0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st}$$ $$x_u = \frac{0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st}}{0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b_f}$$
Same as rectangular beam — width replaced by effective flange width $b_f$

Moment of Resistance:

$$M_u = 0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st} \cdot \left(d - 0.42 \, x_u\right)$$
Or equivalently: $M_u = 0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b_f \cdot x_u \cdot (d - 0.42\,x_u)$

Design Formula for $A_{st}$ (Case 1):

$$A_{st} = \frac{0.5 \, f_{ck}}{f_y} \left[1 - \sqrt{1 - \frac{4.6 \, M_u}{f_{ck} \cdot b_f \cdot d^2}}\right] b_f \cdot d$$
Direct design formula — same as rectangular beam with $b$ replaced by $b_f$

7. Case 2 — Neutral Axis in the Web ($x_u > D_f$)

When the neutral axis is in the web, the compression zone consists of two parts:

  • The full flange of width $b_f$ and thickness $D_f$
  • Part of the web of width $b_w$ from the bottom of the flange to the neutral axis
Fig. 3 — Case 2: stress block split into flange overhangs ($C_1$) and web compression rectangle ($C_2$)

Force Equilibrium (Case 2):

$$C_1 + C_2 = T$$ $$0.446 \, f_{ck} \cdot (b_f - b_w) \cdot y_f + 0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b_w \cdot x_u = 0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st}$$
IS 456:2000, Cl. 38.1 — Case where NA is in the web

Moment of Resistance (Case 2):

$$M_u = 0.446 \, f_{ck} \cdot (b_f - b_w) \cdot y_f \cdot \left(d - \frac{y_f}{2}\right) + 0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b_w \cdot x_u \cdot (d - 0.42 \, x_u)$$
Full moment of resistance for Case 2 — IS 456:2000, Annex G

8. Modified Flange Thickness $y_f$

In Case 2, the stress in the flange overhangs is not uniform — the bottom of the flange near the N.A. carries less stress. IS 456 simplifies this by using a modified (equivalent) flange thickness $y_f$ that gives the same resultant compression force as the actual parabolic-rectangular distribution.

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 38.1, Note
The modified flange thickness $y_f$ is used when $D_f \leq 0.2d$:
$$y_f = 0.15 \, x_u + 0.65 \, D_f \quad \text{(when } D_f > \tfrac{3}{7} x_u \text{)}$$ $$y_f = D_f \quad \text{(when } D_f \leq \tfrac{3}{7} x_u \text{)}$$
$y_f$ = modified flange thickness. The condition $D_f \leq \frac{3}{7}x_u$ means the flange lies entirely within the parabolic part of the stress block; in this case the full $D_f$ is used. IS 456 Cl. 38.1.
Simplified Approach for Design
In most practical T-beam design problems, Case 1 (NA in flange) governs because the large $b_f$ keeps the N.A. high. The Case 2 approach is mainly needed for analysis problems (finding moment capacity of a given section) with a thin flange or heavy steel.

9. Limiting Moment of Resistance

The limiting moment of resistance $M_{u,\lim}$ for a T-beam is found using $x_u = x_{u,\max}$. If Case 1 applies (NA in flange):

$$M_{u,\lim} = 0.36 \frac{x_{u,\max}}{d}\left(1 - 0.42\frac{x_{u,\max}}{d}\right) f_{ck} \cdot b_f \cdot d^2$$
T-beam limiting moment — Case 1 (same form as rectangular with $b_f$)

If $M_u > M_{u,\lim}$, a doubly reinforced T-section is required (compression steel added in the flange/web).

10. Step-by-Step Design Procedure

  • Given Data Span, loads, $f_{ck}$, $f_y$, $b_w$, $D_f$ (slab thickness), c/c beam spacing, exposure condition.
  • Calculate Effective Flange Width $b_f$ Apply IS 456 Cl. 23.1.2 — take the minimum of the three criteria (formula, c/c spacing, actual width).
  • Calculate Factored Moment $M_u$ $w_u = 1.5(w_{DL} + w_{LL})$; $M_u = w_u l^2 / 8$ for SS beam.
  • Assume Overall Depth $D$; Find Effective Depth $d$ Use span/depth thumb rule for preliminary $D$. $d = D - \text{cover} - \phi_{st} - \phi_{main}/2$.
  • Check Case 1 or Case 2 — Assume NA in Flange Find $x_u$ assuming rectangular beam with $b = b_f$: $x_u = \dfrac{0.87 f_y A_{st,\text{trial}}}{0.36 f_{ck} b_f}$. If $x_u \leq D_f$ → Case 1. If $x_u > D_f$ → Case 2.
  • Case 1: Solve for $A_{st}$ Using Rectangular Formula with $b_f$ $A_{st} = \dfrac{0.5 f_{ck}}{f_y}\left[1 - \sqrt{1 - \dfrac{4.6M_u}{f_{ck} b_f d^2}}\right] b_f d$
  • Case 2: Solve $A_{st}$ from Force Equilibrium Find $y_f$, then use: $0.446 f_{ck}(b_f-b_w)y_f + 0.36 f_{ck} b_w x_u = 0.87 f_y A_{st}$. Solve iteratively or by quadratic.
  • Check $x_u \leq x_{u,\max}$; Check Minimum Steel Verify NA is within limit and $A_{st} \geq A_{st,\min} = \dfrac{0.85 b_w d}{f_y}$ (using $b_w$, not $b_f$, for minimum steel).
  • Select Bars and Check Fit in Web Choose bar diameter and number. Bars must fit within $b_w$ with required cover and spacing.
  • Shear Design; Deflection Check; Detailing Sketch Shear: use $b_w$ for $\tau_v$. Deflection: check $l/d$ using $b_w$ for steel percentage modification factor.

11. Worked Example

Problem Statement

Design a T-beam for the following data:

  • Effective span = 6.0 m (simply supported)
  • Centre-to-centre spacing of beams = 3.0 m
  • Slab thickness $D_f$ = 120 mm
  • Web width $b_w$ = 300 mm
  • Overall beam depth $D$ = 550 mm
  • Dead load on beam (excluding self-weight) = 20 kN/m
  • Live load = 25 kN/m
  • Concrete: M25, Steel: Fe 415
  • Exposure: Mild (clear cover = 25 mm)
Step 1 — Effective Flange Width $b_f$

$l_o$ = effective span = 6000 mm (SS beam)

Criterion 1 (IS 456 formula): $b_f = l_o/6 + b_w + 6D_f = 6000/6 + 300 + 6 \times 120 = 1000 + 300 + 720$

$= 2020$ mm

Criterion 2 (c/c spacing): $= 3000$ mm

Criterion 3 (actual width available each side): assumed $= 3000$ mm

$b_f$ = min(2020, 3000, 3000) = 2020 mm
Step 2 — Effective Depth and Factored Moment

Cover = 25 mm, stirrups = 8 mm, main bars = 20 mm.

$d = 550 - 25 - 8 - 10 = $ 507 mm

Self-weight = $0.30 \times (0.55 - 0.12) \times 25 = 0.30 \times 0.43 \times 25$ = 3.23 kN/m

Total DL = 20 + 3.23 = 23.23 kN/m; Total LL = 25 kN/m

$w_u = 1.5 \times (23.23 + 25) = 1.5 \times 48.23$

$w_u$ = 72.35 kN/m

$M_u = \dfrac{72.35 \times 6^2}{8} = \dfrac{72.35 \times 36}{8}$

$M_u$ = 325.6 kN·m
Step 3 — Check Case: Is NA in the Flange?

Assume Case 1 (NA in flange). Use design formula with $b_f = 2020$ mm:

Inner term = $\dfrac{4.6 \times 325.6 \times 10^6}{25 \times 2020 \times 507^2} = \dfrac{1{,}497.8 \times 10^6}{12{,}929 \times 10^6} = 0.1159$

$A_{st} = \dfrac{0.5 \times 25}{415}\left[1 - \sqrt{1-0.1159}\right] \times 2020 \times 507$

$= 0.03012 \times (1 - 0.9381) \times 1{,}024{,}140 = 0.03012 \times 0.0619 \times 1{,}024{,}140$

$A_{st}$ = 1907 mm² (trial)

Check NA depth: $x_u = \dfrac{0.87 \times 415 \times 1907}{0.36 \times 25 \times 2020} = \dfrac{688{,}143}{18{,}180}$

$x_u$ = 37.9 mm

$x_u$ (37.9 mm) $\leq$ $D_f$ (120 mm) ✓ → Case 1 confirmed. NA is in the flange.

Step 4 — Required $A_{st}$ (Case 1 Final)
$A_{st}$ = 1907 mm²

Provide 4 bars of 25 mm dia: $A_{st,\text{prov}} = 4 \times 491 = 1964$ mm² > 1907 mm² ✓

Step 5 — Check Minimum Steel

$A_{st,\min} = \dfrac{0.85 \times b_w \times d}{f_y} = \dfrac{0.85 \times 300 \times 507}{415}$

$A_{st,\min}$ = 311 mm²

1964 mm² > 311 mm² ✓

Step 6 — Check $x_{u,\max}$

$x_{u,\max} = 0.479 \times 507 = 242.9$ mm

Actual $x_u$ with provided steel:

$x_u = \dfrac{0.87 \times 415 \times 1964}{0.36 \times 25 \times 2020} = \dfrac{709{,}235}{18{,}180}$

$x_u$ = 39.0 mm < $x_{u,\max}$ (242.9 mm) ✓ — Under-reinforced, ductile failure mode
Step 7 — Deflection Check

$l/d = 6000/507 = 11.8 \leq 20$ ✓

Deflection OK
Step 8 — Shear Design

$V_u = \dfrac{72.35 \times 6}{2} = 217.1$ kN

$\tau_v = \dfrac{217{,}100}{300 \times 507} = \dfrac{217{,}100}{152{,}100}$

$\tau_v$ = 1.43 MPa

$p_t = \dfrac{100 \times 1964}{300 \times 507} = 1.29\%$. From IS 456 Table 19, M25, $p_t = 1.29\%$: $\tau_c \approx 0.71$ MPa.

$V_{us} = 217100 - 0.71 \times 300 \times 507 = 217100 - 108{,}093 = 109{,}007$ N

Using 2-legged 10 mm stirrups ($A_{sv} = 2 \times 78.5 = 157$ mm²):

$s_v = \dfrac{0.87 \times 415 \times 157 \times 507}{109{,}007} = \dfrac{28{,}731{,}550}{109{,}007}$

$s_v$ = 263 mm → Provide 10 mm 2-legged stirrups @ 250 mm c/c

Max spacing = min(0.75d, 300) = min(380, 300) = 300 mm. Provided 250 mm < 300 mm ✓

Final Design Summary
  • Effective flange width $b_f$: 2020 mm
  • Web width $b_w$: 300 mm | Overall depth $D$: 550 mm | Effective depth $d$: 507 mm
  • Slab thickness $D_f$: 120 mm
  • Neutral axis (actual): 39.0 mm — lies within the flange ✓ (Case 1)
  • Tension steel (bottom): 4 bars of 25 mm dia (Fe 415) — $A_{st}$ = 1964 mm²
  • Stirrups: 10 mm dia, 2-legged @ 250 mm c/c (Fe 415)
  • Clear cover: 25 mm (mild exposure)
  • All IS 456 checks satisfied ✓
