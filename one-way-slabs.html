1. What is a One-Way Slab?

Definition
A one-way slab is a flat RCC structural element that bends and spans in one direction only — from support to support along the shorter span. It is designed as a series of parallel beams of unit width (1 m strip) running in the direction of the shorter span.

One-way slabs are the most common type of slab used in buildings. Examples include floor slabs supported on two parallel walls or beams, staircase landings, roof slabs over rectangular rooms with a high length-to-width ratio, and verandah slabs.

The slab carries main reinforcement (spanning bars) along the short direction, and distribution reinforcement (shrinkage and temperature bars) perpendicular to the span.

2. One-Way vs Two-Way Criterion

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 24.1
Slabs spanning in one direction (one-way slabs) are those in which the ratio of the longer span to shorter span exceeds 2.
$$\frac{l_y}{l_x} > 2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{One-Way Slab}$$ $$\frac{l_y}{l_x} \leq 2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{Two-Way Slab}$$
$l_y$ = longer span, $l_x$ = shorter span — IS 456:2000, Cl. 24.1
Fig. 1 — One-way slab spans in the short direction only; two-way slab spans in both directions
Physical Reason
When $l_y/l_x > 2$, the slab is much stiffer in the short direction and almost all the load is transferred to the two short-side supports. The long sides carry negligible load — hence it is treated as one-way. For a square slab ($l_y/l_x = 1$), load is shared equally in both directions.

3. Load Calculation on Slabs

Slabs carry loads per unit area (kN/m²). The typical loads are:

Load TypeDescriptionTypical Values
Self-weight (Dead) Unit weight of RCC × slab thickness $25 \times D$ kN/m² (D in metres)
Floor finish (Dead) Marble, tiles, screed, etc. 0.5 – 1.5 kN/m²
Partition wall load (Dead) If walls rest on slab 1.0 – 2.0 kN/m²
Live load As per IS 875 Part 2 Residential: 2.0 kN/m²; Office: 3.0–4.0 kN/m²
$$w_u = 1.5 \times (w_{DL} + w_{LL}) \quad \text{kN/m}^2$$
Factored load per unit area — IS 456:2000, Table 18
Converting to Load per Metre on a 1 m Strip
When designing a 1 m wide strip of slab: $w_u$ (kN/m²) × 1 m = $w_u$ (kN/m). The strip is then designed exactly like a simply supported beam of span $l$ and width $b = 1000$ mm.

4. Effective Span

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 22.2
Effective span of a simply supported slab:
$$l_{eff} = \text{min}\left(l_c + d, \; l_c + t_w\right)$$
$l_c$ = clear span; $d$ = effective depth; $t_w$ = support width. Take the lesser value.

In most practical cases for slabs on masonry or concrete walls, the effective span is taken as the centre-to-centre distance of supports or the clear span + effective depth, whichever is less.

Support ConditionEffective Span
Simply supported (SS)Lesser of: $l_c + d$ or $l_c + t_w$ (IS 456 Cl. 22.2a)
Continuous slabCentre-to-centre of supports (IS 456 Cl. 22.2b)
Cantilever$l_c + d/2$ from face of support to free end

5. Depth of Slab — Span/Depth Ratio

The depth of the slab is primarily governed by deflection control, not by moment. IS 456 Cl. 23.2.1 provides basic span-to-effective-depth ratios:

Support ConditionBasic $l/d$ Ratio
Simply supported20
Continuous (interior spans)26
Cantilever7
$$d \geq \frac{l_{eff}}{(l/d)_{\text{basic}} \times M_f}$$
$M_f$ = modification factor for tension steel (from IS 456 Fig. 4) — typically between 1.0 and 2.0. For slabs, $M_f$ is often taken as 1.0–1.4 at the preliminary stage.
Practical Minimum Slab Thickness
  • For simply supported slabs: $D \geq l/20 + \text{cover}$, minimum 100 mm
  • For two-way slabs at edges: minimum cover = 15 mm (mild exposure, IS 456 Cl. 26.4.2.1)
  • For roof slabs exposed to weather: minimum cover = 20 mm

Overall Depth from Effective Depth

$$D = d + \text{clear cover} + \frac{\phi}{2}$$
Minimum clear cover for slabs: 15 mm (mild), 25 mm (moderate) — IS 456 Cl. 26.4.2

6. Design as a 1 m Wide Strip

A one-way slab is designed by cutting a 1 m wide strip perpendicular to the supports and treating it as a simply supported (or continuous) beam. All quantities per metre width are used.

Width of design strip

$b = 1000$ mm (always)

The slab is designed per metre run — all steel areas are in mm²/m.

Moment and Shear

SS slab: $M_u = \dfrac{w_u \cdot l^2}{8}$ kN·m/m

SS slab: $V_u = \dfrac{w_u \cdot l}{2}$ kN/m

$$M_u = \frac{w_u \cdot l_{eff}^2}{8} \quad \text{(kN·m per metre width)}$$
For simply supported one-way slab under uniformly distributed load

7. Main Reinforcement Design

The main reinforcement runs along the short span direction (direction of bending) and is designed to resist the factored bending moment $M_u$.

Using the standard rectangular section formula with $b = 1000$ mm:

$$A_{st} = \frac{0.5 \, f_{ck}}{f_y}\left[1 - \sqrt{1 - \frac{4.6 \, M_u}{f_{ck} \times 1000 \times d^2}}\right] \times 1000 \times d$$
Area of main steel (mm²/m). $M_u$ in N·mm (multiply kN·m by 10⁶).

Spacing of Main Bars

Once $A_{st}$ is found, the spacing of bars of chosen diameter $\phi$ is:

$$s = \frac{a_\phi \times 1000}{A_{st}} \quad \text{mm}$$
$a_\phi = \pi\phi^2/4$ = area of one bar (mm²); $s$ = spacing centre to centre
IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 26.3.3 — Maximum Spacing of Main Bars
Maximum spacing of main reinforcement in slabs shall not exceed the lesser of:
  • 3d or 300 mm — whichever is less

Minimum Steel (IS 456 Cl. 26.5.2.1)

$$A_{st,\min} = 0.12\% \text{ of } bD \quad \text{(for HYSD bars Fe 415, Fe 500)}$$ $$A_{st,\min} = 0.15\% \text{ of } bD \quad \text{(for mild steel Fe 250)}$$
$b = 1000$ mm, $D$ = overall depth. This applies to both main and distribution steel.
Note — Percentage for Slabs vs Beams
The minimum steel percentage for slabs (0.12% of $bD$) is different from that for beams ($0.85/f_y$ of $bd$). Always use the correct formula for slabs — IS 456 Cl. 26.5.2.1.

8. Distribution (Secondary) Reinforcement

Distribution reinforcement runs perpendicular to the main steel (i.e., along the long direction) and serves to:

  • Distribute loads laterally across the slab
  • Resist shrinkage and temperature stresses
  • Tie the main bars in position during construction
IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 26.5.2.1
Area of distribution reinforcement shall not be less than 0.12% of $bD$ (for HYSD) — same as minimum main steel. It is placed above the main steel (closer to the neutral axis).
$$A_{dist,\min} = \frac{0.12}{100} \times 1000 \times D = 1.2 D \quad \text{mm}^2\text{/m}$$
Minimum distribution steel for Fe 415 / Fe 500 slabs ($D$ in mm)
IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 26.3.3 — Maximum Spacing of Distribution Bars
Maximum spacing of distribution bars shall not exceed the lesser of 5d or 450 mm.

Curtailment of Main Bars

For simply supported slabs, alternate main bars may be bent up (cranked) near the supports:

  • 50% of the main bars are bent up at $l/7$ from the face of support
  • These bent-up bars provide additional shear resistance near supports
  • The remaining 50% straight bars are extended to within cover distance from the end
  • All bars must have adequate development length $L_d$ beyond the theoretical cut-off point

10. Shear Check

In slabs, shear reinforcement is generally not provided. The design shear strength of concrete $\tau_c$ must be greater than the nominal shear stress $\tau_v$ from factored loads.

$$\tau_v = \frac{V_u}{b \cdot d} = \frac{V_u}{1000 \times d} \quad \text{(for 1 m wide strip)}$$
Nominal shear stress — IS 456:2000, Cl. 40.1

Find $\tau_c$ from IS 456 Table 19 based on $p_t = \dfrac{100 \, A_{st}}{1000 \times d}$ and grade of concrete.

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 40.2.1
For slabs, the design shear strength of concrete $\tau_c$ shall be multiplied by a factor $k$: $$k \times \tau_c \geq \tau_v$$

Shear Usually Not Critical in Slabs
In practice, one-way slabs rarely fail in shear because: (a) the span-to-depth ratio is controlled by deflection (giving a deeper section), and (b) the $k$ factor boosts $\tau_c$ for thin slabs. If $\tau_v > k\tau_c$, increase slab thickness — do not add stirrups.

11. Step-by-Step Design Procedure

  • Check One-Way or Two-Way Compute $l_y/l_x$. If $> 2$ → one-way slab. Design proceeds along the short span $l_x$.
  • Assume Depth $D$ from Span/Depth Ratio $d \approx l_{eff} / 20$ (SS); $d \approx l_{eff} / 26$ (continuous). Round up $D$ to next 10 mm. Assume cover = 15 mm (mild), main bar dia = 8 or 10 mm.
  • Calculate Effective Span $l_{eff}$ $l_{eff} = \min(l_c + d,\; l_c + t_w)$ for SS slab.
  • Calculate Factored Load $w_u$ Self-weight = $25D$ kN/m². Add floor finish, LL. Apply $\gamma_f = 1.5$. $w_u = 1.5(w_{DL} + w_{LL})$ kN/m².
  • Factored BM and SF (per metre width) $M_u = w_u l_{eff}^2 / 8$; $V_u = w_u l_{eff} / 2$.
  • Check $M_u \leq M_{u,\lim}$ $M_{u,\lim} = R_{u,\lim} \times f_{ck} \times 1000 \times d^2$. If exceeded, increase depth.
  • Calculate Main Steel $A_{st}$ Use direct design formula with $b = 1000$ mm. Check $A_{st} \geq A_{st,\min} = 1.2D$ mm²/m.
  • Find Spacing of Main Bars $s = (a_\phi \times 1000) / A_{st}$. Round down to nearest 5 mm. Check $s \leq \min(3d, 300)$ mm.
  • Calculate Distribution Steel $A_{dist} = 1.2D$ mm²/m (minimum). Find spacing of chosen dia. Check $s \leq \min(5d, 450)$ mm.
  • Shear Check $\tau_v = V_u/(1000d)$. Find $\tau_c$ from IS 456 Table 19, apply $k$ factor. Verify $k\tau_c \geq \tau_v$. If not, increase $D$.
  • Verify Deflection (Span/Depth) Recalculate $l_{eff}/d$ with final depth and check against modified permissible ratio.
  • Draw Detailing Sketch Show: cross-section with main and distribution bars, cover, spacing, cranked bars, overall dimensions.

12. Worked Example

Problem Statement

Design a simply supported one-way slab for the following data:

  • Clear span = 3.5 m
  • Support width = 230 mm (masonry wall)
  • Live load = 3.0 kN/m²
  • Floor finish = 1.0 kN/m²
  • Concrete: M20 ($f_{ck}$ = 20 MPa)
  • Steel: Fe 415 ($f_y$ = 415 MPa)
  • Exposure: Mild (clear cover = 15 mm)
Step 1 — Assume Depth

Basic $l/d$ = 20 (SS). Trial: $d = 3500/20 = 175$ mm.

Assume main bar dia = 10 mm. $D = d + 15 + 5 = 175 + 20 = 195$ mm → Round to $D = 200$ mm.

$D$ = 200 mm; $d$ = 200 − 15 − 5 = 180 mm
Step 2 — Effective Span

$l_c$ = 3500 mm; $d$ = 180 mm; $t_w$ = 230 mm

Option 1: $l_c + d = 3500 + 180 = 3680$ mm

Option 2: $l_c + t_w = 3500 + 230 = 3730$ mm

$l_{eff}$ = min(3680, 3730) = 3680 mm = 3.68 m
Step 3 — Factored Load

Self-weight = $25 \times 0.200 = 5.0$ kN/m²

Floor finish = 1.0 kN/m²

Total DL = 6.0 kN/m². LL = 3.0 kN/m²

$w_u = 1.5 \times (6.0 + 3.0) = 1.5 \times 9.0$

$w_u$ = 13.5 kN/m²
Step 4 — Factored BM and SF (per metre width)

$M_u = \dfrac{13.5 \times 3.68^2}{8} = \dfrac{13.5 \times 13.54}{8} = \dfrac{182.8}{8}$

$M_u$ = 22.85 kN·m/m

$V_u = \dfrac{13.5 \times 3.68}{2} = \dfrac{49.68}{2}$

$V_u$ = 24.84 kN/m
Step 5 — Check Limiting Moment

For M20, Fe 415: $R_{u,\lim} = 0.138$

$M_{u,\lim} = 0.138 \times 20 \times 1000 \times 180^2 = 0.138 \times 20 \times 1000 \times 32400$

$M_{u,\lim}$ = 89.42 kN·m/m

$M_u$ (22.85) $\ll$ $M_{u,\lim}$ (89.42) ✓ — Depth is more than adequate.

Step 6 — Main Steel $A_{st}$

Inner term = $\dfrac{4.6 \times 22.85 \times 10^6}{20 \times 1000 \times 180^2} = \dfrac{105.11 \times 10^6}{648 \times 10^6} = 0.1622$

$\sqrt{1 - 0.1622} = \sqrt{0.8378} = 0.9153$

$A_{st} = \dfrac{0.5 \times 20}{415} \times (1 - 0.9153) \times 1000 \times 180$

$= 0.02410 \times 0.0847 \times 180{,}000 = 0.02410 \times 15{,}246$

$A_{st}$ = 367 mm²/m

Minimum steel = $0.12/100 \times 1000 \times 200 = 240$ mm²/m. Calculated $A_{st}$ (367) > $A_{st,\min}$ (240) ✓

Step 7 — Spacing of Main Bars (10 mm dia)

$a_{10} = \pi \times 100/4 = 78.54$ mm²

$s = \dfrac{78.54 \times 1000}{367} = 214$ mm

Max spacing = min($3d$, 300) = min(540, 300) = 300 mm

Provide 10 mm dia bars @ 210 mm c/c — $A_{st,\text{prov}}$ = 374 mm²/m ✓
Step 8 — Distribution Steel (8 mm dia)

$A_{dist} = 0.12/100 \times 1000 \times 200 = 240$ mm²/m

$a_8 = \pi \times 64/4 = 50.27$ mm²

$s = \dfrac{50.27 \times 1000}{240} = 209$ mm

Max spacing = min($5d$, 450) = min(900, 450) = 450 mm

Provide 8 mm dia bars @ 200 mm c/c — $A_{dist,\text{prov}}$ = 251 mm²/m ✓
Step 9 — Shear Check

$\tau_v = \dfrac{24840}{1000 \times 180} = \dfrac{24840}{180000}$

$\tau_v$ = 0.138 MPa

$p_t = \dfrac{100 \times 374}{1000 \times 180} = 0.208\%$

From IS 456 Table 19, M20, $p_t = 0.208\%$: $\tau_c \approx 0.33$ MPa

$k$ factor for $D = 200$ mm: $k = 1.15$ (IS 456 Cl. 40.2.1)

$k\tau_c = 1.15 \times 0.33 = 0.38$ MPa

$k\tau_c$ (0.38 MPa) > $\tau_v$ (0.138 MPa) ✓ — No shear reinforcement needed
Step 10 — Deflection Check

$l/d = 3680/180 = 20.4 \approx 20$ ✓ (basic ratio for SS = 20; with slight overage acceptable at this low steel percentage since $M_f > 1.0$)

Deflection OK ✓
Final Design Summary
  • Slab thickness $D$: 200 mm
  • Effective depth $d$: 180 mm
  • Main steel (short span): 10 mm dia @ 210 mm c/c (Fe 415) — 374 mm²/m
  • Distribution steel (long span): 8 mm dia @ 200 mm c/c (Fe 415) — 251 mm²/m
  • Clear cover: 15 mm (mild exposure)
  • Cranked bars: 50% of main bars bent up at $l/7$ from face of support
  • All IS 456 checks satisfied ✓
