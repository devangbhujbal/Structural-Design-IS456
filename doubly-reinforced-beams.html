1. When is a Doubly Reinforced Beam Needed?

Definition
A doubly reinforced beam is a rectangular RCC beam in which steel reinforcement is provided in both the tension zone (bottom) and the compression zone (top). The compression steel assists the concrete in carrying compressive forces.

A doubly reinforced beam is required in any of the following situations:

  • The factored moment $M_u$ exceeds the limiting moment $M_{u,\lim}$ of the singly reinforced section — i.e., a singly reinforced beam of the given size cannot carry the load.
  • The beam depth is restricted by architectural or headroom requirements and cannot be increased.
  • The beam is subjected to reversal of moments (e.g., in seismic zones, or at supports of continuous beams where both top and bottom can be in tension at different times).
  • The beam must carry sustained heavy loads — compression steel reduces long-term creep deflection.
Key Trigger
Always check: if $M_u > M_{u,\lim}$, a singly reinforced section is insufficient. The excess moment $M_{u2} = M_u - M_{u,\lim}$ must be carried by the compression steel couple.

2. Concept and Force System

The design of a doubly reinforced beam is best understood by splitting the total moment $M_u$ into two parts:

Part 1 — $M_{u1}$ (Singly reinforced portion)

  • The section acts as a balanced singly reinforced beam
  • Neutral axis at $x_{u,\max}$
  • Moment carried = $M_{u,\lim}$
  • Tension steel required = $A_{st1}$
  • No compression steel involved

Part 2 — $M_{u2}$ (Compression steel couple)

  • Excess moment $M_{u2} = M_u - M_{u,\lim}$
  • Carried by a steel-to-steel couple
  • Compression steel $A_{sc}$ at top (depth $d'$)
  • Additional tension steel $A_{st2}$ at bottom
  • Lever arm = $d - d'$
$$M_u = M_{u1} + M_{u2} = M_{u,\lim} + M_{u2}$$ $$M_{u2} = M_u - M_{u,\lim}$$ $$A_{st} = A_{st1} + A_{st2}$$
Total moment split into two components — the key design concept

4. Strain Compatibility for Compression Steel

Since the neutral axis is at $x_{u,\max}$ (limiting condition), the strain at the compression steel level $d'$ from the top can be found from the linear strain diagram by similar triangles:

$$\varepsilon_{sc} = 0.0035 \times \left(1 - \frac{d'}{x_{u,\max}}\right)$$
Strain in compression steel at the Limit State of Collapse — from linear strain distribution

The stress $f_{sc}$ in the compression steel is then read from the stress-strain curve of the steel at this strain value. For HYSD bars (Fe 415, Fe 500), the stress-strain relationship is non-linear; IS 456 / SP 16 provides tabulated values.

5. Stress in Compression Steel $f_{sc}$

The design stress $f_{sc}$ in compression steel depends on the strain $\varepsilon_{sc}$, which in turn depends on the ratio $d'/d$ (cover to compression steel as a fraction of effective depth). SP 16 provides convenient tables of $f_{sc}$ vs $d'/d$.

Important — Net Compression Force from Steel
The compression steel occupies a region already carrying concrete stress ($0.446 \, f_{ck}$). The net stress contribution from compression steel is therefore: $$f_{sc,\text{net}} = f_{sc} - 0.446 \, f_{ck}$$ This deduction is essential and is frequently missed. IS 456 accounts for it as shown in the force equations below.

6. Force Equilibrium and Moment of Resistance

Three forces act on the section at the Limit State of Collapse:

  • $C_1$ = Compressive force in concrete stress block
  • $C_2$ = Net compressive force in compression steel
  • $T$ = Tensile force in tension steel
$$C_1 = 0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot x_{u,\max}$$ $$C_2 = (f_{sc} - 0.446 \, f_{ck}) \cdot A_{sc}$$ $$T = 0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st}$$
Internal forces in a doubly reinforced beam (IS 456:2000, Cl. 38)

Force Equilibrium ($C_1 + C_2 = T$):

$$0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot x_{u,\max} + (f_{sc} - 0.446 \, f_{ck}) \cdot A_{sc} = 0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st}$$

Total Moment of Resistance:

$$M_u = 0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot x_{u,\max} \cdot (d - 0.42 \, x_{u,\max}) + (f_{sc} - 0.446 \, f_{ck}) \cdot A_{sc} \cdot (d - d')$$
Full moment of resistance of doubly reinforced section — IS 456:2000, Cl. 38.1

Recognising the Two Parts:

$$M_{u1} = 0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot x_{u,\max} \cdot (d - 0.42 \, x_{u,\max}) = M_{u,\lim}$$ $$M_{u2} = (f_{sc} - 0.446 \, f_{ck}) \cdot A_{sc} \cdot (d - d')$$ $$M_u = M_{u1} + M_{u2}$$

7. Design Equations for $A_{sc}$ and $A_{st}$

Compression Steel $A_{sc}$

From the expression for $M_{u2}$:

$$A_{sc} = \frac{M_{u2}}{(f_{sc} - 0.446 \, f_{ck})(d - d')}$$
Where $M_{u2} = M_u - M_{u,\lim}$ and $d' =$ effective cover to compression steel

Tension Steel $A_{st}$

Total tension steel = steel for Part 1 + steel for Part 2:

$$A_{st1} = \frac{M_{u,\lim}}{0.87 \, f_y \, (d - 0.42 \, x_{u,\max})}$$ $$A_{st2} = \frac{M_{u2}}{0.87 \, f_y \, (d - d')}$$ $$\boxed{A_{st} = A_{st1} + A_{st2}}$$
Total tension steel in doubly reinforced beam
Verification by Force Equilibrium
After calculating $A_{sc}$ and $A_{st}$, always verify force equilibrium: $$0.87 \, f_y \cdot A_{st} = 0.36 \, f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot x_{u,\max} + (f_{sc} - 0.446 \, f_{ck}) \cdot A_{sc}$$ If the two sides match, the design is internally consistent.

8. Step-by-Step Design Procedure

  • Given Data Span, loads, $f_{ck}$, $f_y$, $b$, $D$ (restricted), exposure condition.
  • Calculate Factored Moment $M_u$ $w_u = 1.5(w_{DL} + w_{LL})$; $M_u = w_u l^2 / 8$ for SS beam with UDL.
  • Determine Effective Depth $d$ and Cover $d = D - \text{cover} - \phi_{stirrup} - \phi_{main}/2$. Assume cover for exposure condition.
  • Calculate $M_{u,\lim}$ and Check if Doubly Reinforced is Needed $M_{u,\lim} = R_{u,\lim} \cdot f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot d^2$. If $M_u > M_{u,\lim}$, proceed with doubly reinforced design.
  • Find $x_{u,\max}$ and $M_{u2}$ $x_{u,\max} = (x_{u,\max}/d) \times d$; $M_{u2} = M_u - M_{u,\lim}$.
  • Assume $d'$ for Compression Steel and Find $f_{sc}$ Assume effective cover $d'$ to compression steel (typically 40–50 mm). Compute $d'/d$. Read $f_{sc}$ from SP 16 Table F.
  • Calculate Compression Steel $A_{sc}$ $A_{sc} = \dfrac{M_{u2}}{(f_{sc} - 0.446 f_{ck})(d - d')}$
  • Calculate Tension Steel $A_{st}$ $A_{st1} = \dfrac{M_{u,\lim}}{0.87 f_y (d - 0.42 x_{u,\max})}$; $A_{st2} = \dfrac{M_{u2}}{0.87 f_y (d - d')}$; $A_{st} = A_{st1} + A_{st2}$.
  • Verify Force Equilibrium Check: $0.87 f_y A_{st} \approx 0.36 f_{ck} b x_{u,\max} + (f_{sc} - 0.446 f_{ck}) A_{sc}$.
  • Select Bars for $A_{sc}$ and $A_{st}$ Choose diameter and number of bars. Check minimum spacing and width fit.
  • Shear Design Same as singly reinforced — find $V_u$, $\tau_v$, $\tau_c$, design stirrups.
  • Check Minimum and Maximum Steel; Sketch Detailing Verify IS 456 Cl. 26.5.1.1 limits for both tension and compression steel. Draw the detailing sketch.

9. Worked Example

Problem Statement

Design a simply supported doubly reinforced rectangular beam for the following data:

  • Effective span = 6.0 m
  • Factored BM (given directly) = $M_u$ = 320 kN·m
  • Beam size restricted to: $b$ = 300 mm, $D$ = 600 mm
  • Concrete: M25 ($f_{ck}$ = 25 MPa)
  • Steel: Fe 415 ($f_y$ = 415 MPa)
  • Exposure: Mild (clear cover = 25 mm)
Step 1 — Effective Depth

Assume stirrups = 8 mm, main bars = 20 mm.

$d = D - \text{cover} - \phi_{st} - \phi/2 = 600 - 25 - 8 - 10$

$d$ = 557 mm
Step 2 — Limiting Moment of Resistance

For Fe 415: $x_{u,\max}/d = 0.479$, $R_{u,\lim} = 0.138$

$M_{u,\lim} = 0.138 \times 25 \times 300 \times 557^2$

$= 0.138 \times 25 \times 300 \times 310249 = 0.138 \times 2{,}326{,}868{,}000 \times 10^{-6}$

$M_{u,\lim}$ = 321.1 kN·m

$M_u$ (320 kN·m) is just barely $\leq$ $M_{u,\lim}$ (321.1 kN·m). However, this is extremely close — let us proceed as doubly reinforced to demonstrate the full procedure. In practice with this margin you'd keep it singly reinforced, but note that any reduction in depth or increase in load would push it into doubly reinforced territory.

Adjusted Problem for Demonstration
To clearly illustrate the doubly reinforced procedure, let us use $M_u$ = 380 kN·m and $D$ = 600 mm (same section). Now $M_u > M_{u,\lim}$, so doubly reinforced design is required.
Step 3 — Excess Moment $M_{u2}$

$M_{u2} = M_u - M_{u,\lim} = 380 - 321.1$

$M_{u2}$ = 58.9 kN·m
Step 4 — $x_{u,\max}$ and Effective Cover to Compression Steel

$x_{u,\max} = 0.479 \times 557 = 266.8$ mm ≈ 267 mm

Assume effective cover to compression steel: $d' = 25 + 8 + 10 = 43$ mm ≈ 45 mm

$d'/d = 45/557 = 0.081$ ≈ 0.10 (use next higher value from SP 16 Table F)

From SP 16 Table F for Fe 415, $d'/d = 0.10$:

$f_{sc}$ = 353 MPa
Step 5 — Compression Steel $A_{sc}$

Net stress = $f_{sc} - 0.446 f_{ck} = 353 - 0.446 \times 25 = 353 - 11.15 = 341.85$ MPa

Lever arm for compression steel couple = $d - d' = 557 - 45 = 512$ mm

$A_{sc} = \dfrac{M_{u2}}{(f_{sc} - 0.446 f_{ck})(d - d')} = \dfrac{58.9 \times 10^6}{341.85 \times 512}$

$= \dfrac{58{,}900{,}000}{175{,}027}$

$A_{sc}$ = 337 mm²

Provide 2 bars of 16 mm dia: $A_{sc,\text{prov}} = 2 \times 201 = 402$ mm² > 337 mm² ✓

Step 6 — Tension Steel $A_{st}$

Part 1 (for $M_{u,\lim}$):

$A_{st1} = \dfrac{M_{u,\lim}}{0.87 f_y (d - 0.42 x_{u,\max})} = \dfrac{321.1 \times 10^6}{0.87 \times 415 \times (557 - 0.42 \times 267)}$

$= \dfrac{321.1 \times 10^6}{361.05 \times (557 - 112.1)} = \dfrac{321.1 \times 10^6}{361.05 \times 444.9}$

$= \dfrac{321.1 \times 10^6}{160{,}644}$

$A_{st1}$ = 1999 mm²

Part 2 (for $M_{u2}$):

$A_{st2} = \dfrac{M_{u2}}{0.87 f_y (d - d')} = \dfrac{58.9 \times 10^6}{361.05 \times 512} = \dfrac{58{,}900{,}000}{184{,}858}$

$A_{st2}$ = 319 mm²

Total: $A_{st} = 1999 + 319$

$A_{st}$ = 2318 mm²

Provide 5 bars of 25 mm dia: $A_{st,\text{prov}} = 5 \times 491 = 2455$ mm² > 2318 mm² ✓

Step 7 — Force Equilibrium Check

Compression = $C_1 + C_2$:

$C_1 = 0.36 \times 25 \times 300 \times 267 = 723{,}780$ N

$C_2 = (353 - 11.15) \times 402 = 341.85 \times 402 = 137{,}424$ N

Total compression = $723{,}780 + 137{,}424 = 861{,}204$ N

Tension = $T = 0.87 \times 415 \times 2455 = 885{,}877$ N

861,204 ≈ 885,877 N ✓ (Difference ~2.8% — acceptable, due to providing slightly more steel than required)
Step 8 — Deflection Check

$l/d = 6000/557 = 10.77$

10.77 < 20 (basic ratio for SS beam) ✓
Final Design Summary
  • Size: 300 mm × 600 mm (b × D)
  • Effective depth: 557 mm
  • Tension steel (bottom): 5 bars of 25 mm dia (Fe 415) — $A_{st}$ = 2455 mm²
  • Compression steel (top): 2 bars of 16 mm dia (Fe 415) — $A_{sc}$ = 402 mm²
  • Stirrups: Design as per shear force (8 mm 2-legged @ spacing as required)
  • Clear cover: 25 mm (mild exposure)
  • All IS 456 checks satisfied ✓

10. Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the errors that most often cost marks in exams and cause re-work on site. Watch for them when applying this topic.

Common MistakeWhy it’s wrong / the correct approach
Providing compression steel when it isn’t required.If $M_u \le M_{u,\lim}$ a singly reinforced section works and is cheaper. Doubly reinforced design is justified only when $M_u > M_{u,\lim}$ or the depth is restricted.
Using $0.87 f_y$ as the compression-steel stress.The stress $f_{sc}$ in compression steel is limited by its strain (governed by $d'/d$) and is usually less than $0.87 f_y$ — read it from the $d'/d$ table (SP 16).
Ignoring the concrete displaced by the compression bars.The compression-steel force should use $(f_{sc} - 0.446 f_{ck})$, subtracting the concrete that the bars replace, otherwise capacity is overstated.
Not splitting the tension steel into $A_{st1}$ and $A_{st2}$.$A_{st1}$ balances $M_{u,\lim}$ (with concrete) and $A_{st2}$ balances the extra moment (with compression steel). Total $A_{st} = A_{st1} + A_{st2}$.
Leaving compression bars without restraint.Compression bars can buckle. They must be enclosed by ties at spacing not exceeding the limits of IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3.2 (as for columns).
Using the wrong $d'/d$ ratio.$d'$ is the cover to the compression steel centroid. A larger $d'/d$ lowers $f_{sc}$ significantly, so it must be estimated correctly before design.

11. Frequently Asked Questions

When is a doubly reinforced beam needed?

When the factored moment exceeds the limiting moment of the section ($M_u > M_{u,\lim}$) — typically because the depth is architecturally restricted.

Why is $f_{sc}$ less than $0.87 f_y$?

Because the compression steel sits close to the neutral axis, its strain may be below the yield strain. The stress is therefore read from the strain-compatibility table as a function of $d'/d$.

What are $A_{st1}$ and $A_{st2}$?

$A_{st1}$ is the tension steel that pairs with the concrete to resist $M_{u,\lim}$; $A_{st2}$ is the additional tension steel that pairs with the compression steel to resist the balance $M_u - M_{u,\lim}$.

Do the compression bars need stirrups around them?

Yes — they must be tied like column bars to prevent buckling, per IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3.2.

Is a doubly reinforced beam economical?

Only when genuinely required. Adding compression steel and the matching tension steel is more expensive than simply deepening the beam where space allows.
