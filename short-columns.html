1. What is a Column?

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 25.1
A column is a compression member, the effective length of which exceeds three times the least lateral dimension. Members with an effective length less than three times the least lateral dimension are called pedestals.

Columns are vertical structural members that transfer loads from beams and slabs down to the foundations. They primarily carry axial compressive loads, often combined with bending moments due to frame action, eccentricity of load, or lateral forces (wind/seismic).

The design of columns is governed by IS 456 : 2000, Clause 39 — Limit State of Collapse in Compression.

2. Short vs Slender Columns

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 25.1.2
A column is classified as short when both the slenderness ratios about the two principal axes are less than 12: $$\frac{l_{ex}}{D} < 12 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{l_{ey}}{b} < 12$$ If either ratio exceeds 12, the column is slender (long).

Effective Length $l_e$

The effective length depends on end conditions (fixed, pinned, free). IS 456 Table 28 gives effective length factors. For typical framed columns:

3. Types of Columns by Reinforcement

Tied Column (Rectangular / Square)

  • Longitudinal bars held in position by lateral ties (rectangular stirrups)
  • Most common type in building construction
  • Ties prevent buckling of longitudinal bars
  • IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3.2 governs tie spacing and dia

Helically Reinforced Column (Circular)

  • Longitudinal bars enclosed by a closely wound helix (spiral)
  • Helix confines the concrete core — greatly increases ductility
  • Higher load capacity than tied column of same size
  • IS 456 allows 5% increase in load capacity (Cl. 39.4)

4. Minimum Eccentricity

In practice, a perfectly axially loaded column never exists — there are always small imperfections in construction, load application, and alignment. IS 456 therefore requires that every column be designed for a minimum eccentricity.

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 25.4
All columns shall be designed for a minimum eccentricity equal to the unsupported length of the column divided by 500, plus the lateral dimension divided by 30, subject to a minimum of 20 mm:
$$e_{\min} = \frac{l}{500} + \frac{D}{30} \geq 20 \text{ mm}$$
$l$ = unsupported length of column (mm); $D$ = lateral dimension in the plane of bending (mm)
When IS 456 Cl. 39.3 Applies
If the eccentricity $e_{\min}$ does not exceed $0.05D$, the simplified axial load formula (Cl. 39.3) may be used. If $e_{\min} > 0.05D$, the column must be designed as an eccentrically loaded member using interaction diagrams (SP 16).

5. Axially Loaded Short Columns

For a short column with eccentricity not exceeding $0.05D$, IS 456 Cl. 39.3 gives the design load capacity:

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 39.3
When the minimum eccentricity does not exceed 0.05 times the lateral dimension, the following formula applies:
$$P_u = 0.4 \, f_{ck} \cdot A_c + 0.67 \, f_y \cdot A_{sc}$$
Design axial load capacity of short column — IS 456:2000, Cl. 39.3

Where:

  • $P_u$ = factored axial load (N)
  • $A_c$ = area of concrete = $A_g - A_{sc}$ (mm²)
  • $A_g$ = gross cross-sectional area of column (mm²)
  • $A_{sc}$ = total area of longitudinal steel (mm²)
  • $f_{ck}$ = characteristic compressive strength of concrete (MPa)
  • $f_y$ = yield strength of steel (MPa)
Use $A_c = A_g - A_{sc}$, not $A_g$
The area of concrete $A_c$ excludes the steel area. For a first trial, you may use $A_c \approx A_g$ (since $A_{sc}$ is typically 1–4% of $A_g$), then refine.

Rearranged for Design (finding $A_{sc}$):

$$A_{sc} = \frac{P_u - 0.4 \, f_{ck} \cdot A_g}{0.67 f_y - 0.4 f_{ck}}$$
Design formula to find required area of longitudinal steel. Assumes $A_c \approx A_g$ initially.

For Helically Reinforced Columns (IS 456 Cl. 39.4):

$$P_u = 1.05 \times \left(0.4 \, f_{ck} \cdot A_c + 0.67 \, f_y \cdot A_{sc}\right)$$
5% increase in load capacity for helically reinforced columns — IS 456 Cl. 39.4

6. Eccentrically Loaded Short Columns

When a column carries both axial load $P_u$ and bending moment $M_u$ (i.e., eccentricity $e = M_u/P_u > 0.05D$), it must be designed using the interaction diagram method from SP 16.

The interaction diagram (P-M diagram) defines the boundary of all combinations of $P_u$ and $M_u$ that the column can safely carry. Any point inside the diagram is safe; any point outside represents failure.

Fig. 2 — P-M interaction diagram. The design point ($P_u$, $M_u$) must lie inside (or on) the curve.
Using SP 16 Charts for Eccentric Columns
SP 16 (Design Aids for IS 456) provides non-dimensional interaction charts (Charts 27–62) for rectangular and circular sections. The procedure is:
  1. Compute $P_u/(f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot D)$ and $M_u/(f_{ck} \cdot b \cdot D^2)$
  2. Assume $p/f_{ck}$ (steel percentage / $f_{ck}$), choose appropriate chart for $d'/D$
  3. Read off $p$ from the chart; find $A_{sc} = p \times b \times D / 100$
This site covers the axially loaded case in full; eccentric column design using SP 16 will be covered in a dedicated advanced page.

7. Steel Limits — IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3

Minimum Longitudinal Steel

IS 456 : 2000 — Clause 26.5.3.1(a)
$$A_{sc,\min} = 0.8\% \text{ of } A_g$$
Minimum longitudinal steel to resist unexpected eccentricity and handle creep/shrinkage

Maximum Longitudinal Steel

$$A_{sc,\max} = 6\% \text{ of } A_g \quad \text{(at laps: } 4\%\text{)}$$
IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3.1(b). At splice locations, the limit is reduced to 4% to allow lapping.

Minimum Number of Bars

Column ShapeMinimum Bars
Rectangular4 bars (one at each corner)
Circular6 bars
Polygonal (any shape)One bar at each corner/vertex

Minimum Bar Diameter

$$\phi_{\min} = 12 \text{ mm}$$
IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3.1(c) — minimum diameter of longitudinal bars in columns

8. Lateral Ties / Stirrups — IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3.2

Lateral ties prevent buckling of longitudinal bars and confine the concrete core. The diameter and spacing must satisfy:

Diameter of Ties (IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3.2(b)):

$$\phi_{tie} \geq \max\left(\frac{\phi_{main}}{4},\; 6 \text{ mm}\right)$$
Tie diameter shall not be less than one-quarter of the largest longitudinal bar or 6 mm, whichever is greater

Spacing of Ties (IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3.2(c)):

$$s_v \leq \min\left(b_{\text{least}},\; 16\,\phi_{\min,\text{long}},\; 300 \text{ mm}\right)$$
Pitch of lateral ties shall not exceed least lateral dimension, 16× smallest longitudinal bar dia, or 300 mm
Practical Tie Arrangement
  • Every alternate longitudinal bar shall have a tie passing around it (for columns with bars on all four faces)
  • No bar shall be farther than 150 mm from a restrained bar (IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3.2(d))
  • For bars in a circular arrangement, a single circular tie is sufficient

9. Helically Reinforced Columns

The helix must be designed to provide adequate confinement. IS 456 Cl. 39.4.1 specifies the minimum ratio of volume of helical steel to volume of core concrete:

$$\frac{V_{hs}}{V_c} \geq 0.36 \left(\frac{A_g}{A_c} - 1\right) \frac{f_{ck}}{f_y}$$
Volumetric ratio of helix to core concrete — IS 456:2000, Cl. 39.4.1. $A_g$ = gross area, $A_c$ = core area.

Pitch of Helix (IS 456 Cl. 26.5.3.2(a)):

$$s_h \leq \min\left(\frac{D_c}{6},\; 75 \text{ mm}\right) \quad \text{and} \quad s_h \geq 25 \text{ mm or } 3\phi_{helix}$$
$D_c$ = diameter of core (to outside of helix); $\phi_{helix}$ = diameter of helix bar

11. Step-by-Step Design Procedure

For a short column with axial load only (or with $e_{\min} \leq 0.05D$).

  • Given Data Factored axial load $P_u$, unsupported length $l$, grade of concrete and steel, exposure condition.
  • Assume Column Size Choose $b$ and $D$ (or diameter for circular). Check $l_e/D < 12$ to confirm it is a short column.
  • Check Minimum Eccentricity $e_{\min} = l/500 + D/30 \geq 20$ mm. If $e_{\min} \leq 0.05D$, use Cl. 39.3 formula.
  • Find Required $A_{sc}$ $A_{sc} = \dfrac{P_u - 0.4 f_{ck} A_g}{0.67 f_y - 0.4 f_{ck}}$
  • Check Min and Max Steel Limits $0.8\% A_g \leq A_{sc} \leq 6\% A_g$. Adjust column size if needed.
  • Select Bars Choose dia $\phi$ and number $n$ such that $n \cdot a_\phi \geq A_{sc}$. Min 4 bars (rect.) or 6 bars (circular). Min dia 12 mm.
  • Verify Load Capacity with Provided Steel $P_{u,\text{prov}} = 0.4 f_{ck}(A_g - A_{sc,\text{prov}}) + 0.67 f_y \cdot A_{sc,\text{prov}} \geq P_u$ ✓
  • Design Lateral Ties Dia: $\max(\phi_{main}/4, 6$ mm$)$. Spacing: $\min(b, 16\phi_{min,long}, 300$ mm$)$.
  • Draw Detailing Sketch Show cross-section: longitudinal bar positions, tie arrangement, cover, and dimensions.

12. Worked Example 1 — Axially Loaded Column

Problem Statement

Design a short rectangular column for the following data:

  • Factored axial load $P_u$ = 1500 kN
  • Unsupported length $l$ = 3.0 m
  • Concrete: M25 ($f_{ck}$ = 25 MPa)
  • Steel: Fe 415 ($f_y$ = 415 MPa)
  • Exposure: Mild (clear cover = 40 mm)
  • Assume square section: $b = D$
Step 1 — Assume Column Size

Trial: assume 2% steel. $P_u = 0.4 f_{ck} A_g + (0.67 f_y - 0.4 f_{ck}) \times 0.02 A_g$

$1500 \times 10^3 = A_g [0.4 \times 25 + (0.67 \times 415 - 0.4 \times 25) \times 0.02]$

$= A_g [10 + (278.05 - 10) \times 0.02] = A_g [10 + 5.361] = 15.361 A_g$

$A_g = 1500000/15.361 = 97,650$ mm²

Side = $\sqrt{97650} = 312$ mm → round up to $b = D = 350$ mm

Try 350 mm × 350 mm column; $A_g$ = 122,500 mm²
Step 2 — Check Short Column

Effective length $l_e = 0.65 \times 3000 = 1950$ mm (both ends fixed condition assumed)

$l_e/D = 1950/350 = 5.57 < 12$ ✓

Short column confirmed ✓
Step 3 — Minimum Eccentricity

$e_{\min} = \dfrac{l}{500} + \dfrac{D}{30} = \dfrac{3000}{500} + \dfrac{350}{30} = 6 + 11.67 = 17.67$ mm

But minimum = 20 mm → $e_{\min}$ = 20 mm

$0.05D = 0.05 \times 350 = 17.5$ mm

$e_{\min}$ (20 mm) > 0.05D (17.5 mm) → technically needs eccentric design, but margin is very small. Proceed with Cl. 39.3 as a practical approximation and note this.

In practice for very small eccentricity beyond 0.05D, a slightly conservative column size is chosen. Here we increase to 375 mm × 375 mm.

Step 3 (Revised) — 375 mm × 375 mm

$A_g = 375 \times 375 = 140,625$ mm²

$e_{\min} = 6 + 350/30 → $ use $D = 375$: $e_{\min} = 6 + 375/30 = 6 + 12.5 = 18.5$ mm → use 20 mm

$0.05D = 0.05 \times 375 = 18.75$ mm

$e_{\min}$ (20 mm) > 0.05D (18.75 mm) → still slightly above. Increase to 400 mm × 400 mm.
Step 3 (Final) — 400 mm × 400 mm

$A_g = 160,000$ mm²; $0.05D = 0.05 \times 400 = 20$ mm

$e_{\min} = 3000/500 + 400/30 = 6 + 13.33 = 19.33$ mm < 20 mm → use 20 mm

$e_{\min}$ = 20 mm = $0.05D$ = 20 mm ✓ — Cl. 39.3 applies exactly.
Step 4 — Required Longitudinal Steel

$A_{sc} = \dfrac{P_u - 0.4 f_{ck} A_g}{0.67 f_y - 0.4 f_{ck}} = \dfrac{1500000 - 0.4 \times 25 \times 160000}{0.67 \times 415 - 0.4 \times 25}$

$= \dfrac{1500000 - 1600000}{278.05 - 10}$

Numerator is negative → column is oversized. $P_u$ < $0.4 f_{ck} A_g$, which means concrete alone is nearly sufficient. Apply minimum steel.

$A_{sc,\min} = 0.8\% \times 160000 = 1280$ mm²

Use minimum steel: $A_{sc}$ = 1280 mm²
Step 5 — Verify Load Capacity

Provide 4 bars of 20 mm dia: $A_{sc,\text{prov}} = 4 \times 314.16 = 1257$ mm²

Since 1257 < 1280 mm², try 4 bars of 20 mm + 2 bars of 12 mm:

$A_{sc,\text{prov}} = 4 \times 314.16 + 2 \times 113.1 = 1256.6 + 226.2 = 1483$ mm² > 1280 ✓

$P_{u,\text{prov}} = 0.4 \times 25 \times (160000 - 1483) + 0.67 \times 415 \times 1483$

$= 0.4 \times 25 \times 158517 + 278.05 \times 1483$

$= 1585170 + 412388 = 1997558$ N

$P_{u,\text{prov}}$ = 1997.6 kN > $P_u$ (1500 kN) ✓

The column is comfortably over-designed — this is expected when minimum steel governs and the section was sized for eccentricity conditions. In practice, a slightly smaller section (350 mm × 350 mm) using proper eccentric design via SP 16 would be more economical.

Step 6 — Lateral Ties

Main bar dia = 20 mm. Tie dia = max(20/4, 6) = max(5, 6) = 6 mm → use 8 mm (next standard size).

Spacing = min($b$, $16\phi_{min}$, 300) = min(400, 16×12, 300) = min(400, 192, 300)

Tie spacing = 190 mm c/c (round down from 192 mm). Provide 8 mm ties @ 190 mm c/c.
Final Design Summary — Example 1
  • Column size: 400 mm × 400 mm
  • Longitudinal steel: 4 bars of 20 mm + 2 bars of 12 mm (Fe 415) — $A_{sc}$ = 1483 mm²
  • Lateral ties: 8 mm dia @ 190 mm c/c
  • Clear cover: 40 mm (mild exposure)
  • Load capacity: 1997.6 kN > 1500 kN ✓
  • All IS 456 checks satisfied ✓

13. Worked Example 2 — Given Section, Find Load Capacity

Problem Statement

A 300 mm × 400 mm short column is reinforced with 6 bars of 20 mm dia (Fe 415). Concrete grade M25. Find the safe load capacity $P_u$.

Step 1 — Section Properties

$A_g = 300 \times 400 = 120,000$ mm²

$A_{sc} = 6 \times \dfrac{\pi \times 400}{4} = 6 \times 314.16 = 1885$ mm²

$A_c = 120000 - 1885 = 118,115$ mm²

$A_{sc}$ = 1885 mm² = 1.57% of $A_g$ (between 0.8% and 6%) ✓
Step 2 — Check Eccentricity (assume $l$ = 3 m, both ends fixed)

$l_e = 0.65 \times 3000 = 1950$ mm

$l_e/D = 1950/400 = 4.875 < 12$ ✓ — Short column

$e_{\min} = 3000/500 + 400/30 = 6 + 13.33 = 19.33$ mm → use 20 mm

$0.05D = 0.05 \times 400 = 20$ mm

$e_{\min}$ (20 mm) $\leq$ $0.05D$ (20 mm) ✓ — IS 456 Cl. 39.3 applies
Step 3 — Load Capacity

$P_u = 0.4 f_{ck} \cdot A_c + 0.67 f_y \cdot A_{sc}$

$= 0.4 \times 25 \times 118115 + 0.67 \times 415 \times 1885$

$= 1{,}181{,}150 + 524{,}448$

$P_u$ = 1,705,598 N = 1705.6 kN
Result — Example 2

A 300 mm × 400 mm column with 6–20 mm bars (Fe 415) in M25 concrete has a safe load capacity of 1705.6 kN under axial loading with minimum eccentricity.
